Section 2: Sample Preparation
The Circle of Cryo-EM Sample Preparation
Four key stages of sample preparation for cryo-EM
Sample Optimization
Maintain native state: pH, salt, detergents, cross-linking. Screen by negative stain before cryo-EM.
Sample Carriers & Treatments
3mm grid (Cu/Au mesh + holey foil). Plasma clean for wettability. Continuous film for particle distribution.
Deposition
Form 10–100 nm thin film on grid. Air-water interface causes denaturation — minimize deposition-to-freezing time.
Vitrification
Flash-freeze in liquid ethane (>10⁵ K/s) to form vitreous ice. Store below −137°C to prevent devitrification.
Weissenberger et al., Nat. Methods (2021)

Section 2: Sample Preparation
Cryo-EM Grid
A 3 mm support that holds the sample as a thin film of vitreous ice
1
Grid / metal mesh
Usually Cu, Au, or Ni; Ti and other metals are specialty options. Common mesh counts are 200, 300, or 400.
2
Foil / thin support layer
A thin perforated support, typically carbon or gold; formats include holey, lacey, HexAuFoil, and continuous films.
3
Hole / imaging window
Vitreous ice forms across the holes; hole/spacing choices such as 1.2/1.3, 2/1, and 0.6/1 µm tune the imaging area.
4
Support film / optional surface
Carbon, graphene, or graphene oxide can improve distribution and orientation, but may add background.

Grid Handling
A grid is a fragile mechanical support, not just a sample holder
Do not grip inside the support
  • The foil and mesh deform easily.
  • Visible wrinkles mean the grid was damaged before vitrification.
Use the rim-only grip
  • Use sharp, straight tweezers.
  • Pick up supports by the rim only.
Reduce static and carry-over
  • Static can move or damage delicate supports.
  • Tweezer residue can contaminate the next grid.
  • Use glass dishes, an anti-static setup, and clean tweezers.
Passmore & Russo, Methods Enzymol. 579, 51-86 (2016), Figs. 3-4 · DOI: 10.1016/bs.mie.2016.04.011 · LMB EM course 2017, Lori Passmore

Automated Grid Preparation
Blot-free and low-volume systems reduce manual variability and air-water-interface exposure
Inkjet + self-wicking
Chameleon
  • Piezo inkjet applies picoliter droplets to the grid.
  • Self-wicking nanowires remove excess liquid without filter-paper blotting.
  • Fast plunge timing reduces exposure at the air-water interface.
Commercial
VitroJet
  • Pin-writing deposits liquid without a spray nozzle.
  • Integrated workflow: plasma treatment, deposition, vitrification, gridbox storage.
  • Parameters: layer thickness, covered area, and grid hydrophilicity can be tuned.
Emerging system
cryoWriter
  • Microcapillary writing uses only a few nanoliters of sample.
  • Climate control tunes humidity and temperature around the grid.
  • Modular workflow can combine grid preparation with on-grid manipulation.
Research prototype
EasyGrid / VitriFlex
  • EasyGrid: in-line plasma treatment, blotless liquid removal, jet vitrification, cryo-storage.
  • VitriFlex: open-source SCARA robot platform for programmable grid prep.
  • Shared aim: modular SPA, cryo-ET, mixing, and time-resolved workflows.

Optimization logic
Match the intervention to the failure mode

Practical levers
1
Different vitrification
Change blotting, spraying, or self-wicking physics.
2
Functionalized grids
Use affinity, lipid, charged, or support-film interactions.
3
Longer collection
Collect more movies when usable particle yield is low.
4
Tilted collection
Recover missing views when orientation bias remains.
